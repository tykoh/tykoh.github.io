The start of a race is easy to love. You're fresh, your kit is clean, the crowd is there, and everything is still possible. The finish line has its own energy — the accumulated momentum of everything you've been through, the certainty that it's nearly over.

The middle is something else.

In a 100-kilometre race, the middle is roughly kilometre 30 to kilometre 70. You're too far in to turn back, too far out to see the end. The crowd is gone. Your legs have started the long conversation with your brain about whether this was a reasonable idea. And you have approximately four to six hours left, in conditions that are no longer novel.

This is where most things fail. Not at the start, where enthusiasm covers for a lot. Not at the end, where the finish line pulls you forward. In the middle, where there's nothing to do but keep going because you've decided you're going to keep going.

---

## Projects have a middle too

I've noticed the same topology in every significant project I've worked on.

The start is energising. New team, new problem, possibility in every direction. People bring their best ideas to kickoffs. The architecture diagrams are clean. The product roadmap has not yet met reality.

The finish, when it comes, has its own satisfaction. The launch, the retrospective, the team celebration. You're proud of what you shipped, even if it's not exactly what you imagined at the start.

The middle is the refactoring you planned but keep deferring. The technical debt that accumulates in the parts of the system no one wants to touch. The team morale that dips in month four when the initial excitement has faded but the product hasn't shipped yet. The roadmap that's been reprioritised three times and now barely resembles the original.

The middle is where projects lose their shape. Where teams stop believing in the outcome. Where shortcuts start to look like reasonable decisions. Where the person who had the vision at the start is no longer sure what success looks like.

---

## What gets you through it

In endurance sport, there's a concept called "chunking" — breaking a long race into manageable segments rather than thinking about the whole thing at once. You don't run a hundred kilometres. You run to the next checkpoint. Then the one after that. The whole becomes approachable by making it small.

This works for two reasons. First, it reduces the cognitive weight of the remaining distance — you're not carrying the full 70 kilometres still ahead of you, just the 8 kilometres to the next aid station. Second, it gives you regular small completions. Forward progress. Evidence that the thing is possible.

In project work, I've found the equivalent is having a clear definition of what "done" looks like at a smaller scale than the overall project. Not just a launch milestone, but working software every two weeks. Not just a platform that's production-ready, but a specific team that can deploy their first service without help by the end of the sprint. Small, concrete, verifiable progress that doesn't require the project to be finished to be meaningful.

The teams that struggle in the middle are often the ones that have their success defined entirely by the outcome — the launch, the release, the platform adoption number. When that outcome is six months away and the middle is hard, there's nothing to hold on to.

## On commitment

There's a harder truth about the middle, which is that it's where commitment is tested rather than assumed.

At the start, commitment is easy. Everyone's on board. The decision to do the thing was recent and enthusiastic. There's no accumulated frustration to colour it.

In the middle, commitment means something different. It means staying in when it would be reasonable to leave. Keeping the scope honest when it would be easier to expand it because the original scope is harder than expected. Making the unglamorous decisions — fixing the observability before adding features, addressing the engineering hire you've been deferring, having the direct conversation with the stakeholder who's been redefining success every two weeks.

I've learned that the teams and projects that navigate the middle well share one quality: they made an explicit commitment at the start that they can refer back to when things get hard. Not just a goal, but an agreement — with each other, with themselves — about what they're doing and why, and what they'll do when it stops being fun.

That agreement is what you draw on in the middle. Not enthusiasm (that's gone). Not momentum (that varies). The agreement.

---

## It always ends

The practical thing to say about the middle is that it ends. Every race I've run, there was a point where I thought I wouldn't finish, and I finished. Every project I've been part of that felt like it had lost its way eventually either shipped or was explicitly cancelled — and both endings were better than the indefinite middle.

The middle is not a permanent state. It just feels like one when you're in it.

The discipline is remembering, in the middle, that you made a decision before the middle started — a decision made with clearer information about who you are and what you're capable of than you have access to at kilometre 55, when your quads have forgotten what flat ground feels like.

Trust that decision. Keep going to the next checkpoint. The middle ends.
